From Lines to Circles

Beyond "Take-Make-Waste"

Our current economic system largely follows a linear model: we extract resources, make products, and discard them as waste. This "take-make-waste" approach assumes infinite resources and somewhere to put our growing mountains of wasteâ€”assumptions our planet can no longer support.

The circular economy offers a different vision. It mimics natural systems where nothing is wastedâ€”one organism's waste becomes another's food. In a circular economy, products are designed to be reused, repaired, refurbished, remanufactured, and recycled, keeping materials in use for as long as possible.

Linear vs. Circular Economy

Linear Economy
Resources are extracted, transformed into products, used briefly, then discarded as waste.
Circular Economy
Products and materials are kept in use, regenerating natural systems rather than depleting them.

Circular thinking isn't new to Africa. Traditional practices across the continent have long embraced principles of reuse and regeneration:

South Africa's Circular Pioneers
  • EcoPlastile transforms plastic waste into paving tiles, creating jobs while addressing waste
  • Reform Africa converts plastic waste into waterproof, durable school bags for children
  • The Kampala Jellitex Initiative transforms textile waste into new clothing and accessories
  • Rethaka's Repurpose Schoolbags are manufactured from recycled plastic bags with integrated solar panels that charge during the walk to school, providing light for evening studying
  • The Litterboom Project has installed simple but effective floating barriers across rivers in KwaZulu-Natal that have captured over 160,000 kg of plastic waste
  • Use-It converts waste materials into eco-friendly building materials and creates green jobs in the process

Core Activity: Circular Solutions Workshop

60-75 minutes
Groups of 3-5
Moderate

Materials Needed:

  • Common plastic items (bottles, bags, containers, etc.)â€”one per group
  • Large paper or cardboard sheets (reused packaging works well)
  • Markers or colored pencils
  • Reference images of circular economy examples (provided below)
  • Use natural materials to represent circular systems (seeds, leaves arranged in circles)
  • Create model circular systems using entirely locally sourced materials
  • Use oral storytelling traditions to explain circular concepts
  • Draw in dirt or sand using sticks to create circular diagrams

Activity Steps

1

Exploring the Models (15 minutes)

Start by physically demonstrating three economic models:

  • Linear Economy: Stand in a line, passing an object from "extraction" through "production," "use," to "disposal"
  • Recycling Economy: Form a line that curves back at the end, with some material returning to production
  • Circular Economy: Form a circle, demonstrating how materials continually flow through the system

Discuss the differences between these models and their environmental impacts.

2

Circular Inspiration (15 minutes)

In small groups, examine examples of circular solutions from Uganda and South Africa. For each example, answer:

  • What problem does this solution address?
  • How does it keep materials in use?
  • What value does it create (environmental, social, economic)?
  • How does it connect to local culture or knowledge?
EcoPlastile's plastic pavers made from waste
Repurpose Schoolbags with integrated solar panels
3

Circular Redesign Challenge (25 minutes)

Select a common plastic item and redesign both the product and its system to be truly circular. Consider:

  • Product design: How could it be made to last longer or be more easily repaired/recycled?
  • Business model: Could it be offered as a service instead of a product?
  • Materials: What sustainable materials could replace plastic?
  • End-of-life: How could components be separated and reused?

Create a visual representation of your circular solution on large paper.

4

Solution Showcase (15-20 minutes)

Present your circular redesign to the class. After each presentation, other students offer:

  • One "star" (something they liked)
  • One "wish" (suggestion for improvement)

As a class, vote on which solutions seem most feasible for local implementation.
