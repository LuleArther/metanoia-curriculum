Beyond What We Can See

The Power of Systems Thinking

When we look at environmental challenges like plastic pollution, we often focus only on what's immediately visibleâ€”the plastic bottle on the beach, the clogged drainage ditch, or the overflowing waste bin. But these visible problems are just the "tip of the iceberg." Beneath the surface lie the patterns, structures, and mindsets that keep creating these problems, even when we try to address the symptoms.

Systems thinking gives us tools to see below the surface and understand the whole picture. Rather than just picking up plastic waste (which is still important!), we can begin to ask: Why is this waste being generated in the first place? What systems are in place that make plastic the default choice? What beliefs or mental models make single-use seem normal and acceptable?

The Iceberg Model: A Tool for Deeper Understanding

The Iceberg Model helps us analyze problems at four levels:

  1. Events (Tip of the iceberg): The visible incidents we can easily see
  2. Patterns (Just below the surface): Trends that occur over time
  3. Structures (Deeper down): Policies, systems, and physical infrastructures that create the patterns
  4. Mental Models (Deep underwater): The beliefs, assumptions, and values that create and sustain these structures

Example: Plastic Waste in Uganda and South Africa

Level Uganda Example South Africa Example
Events Plastic bags clogging drainage channels in Kampala Plastic bottles washing up on Cape Town beaches after storms
Patterns Increased flooding during rainy seasons; rising use of kaveera bags for packaging Seasonal spikes in beach pollution; declining fish populations in certain areas
Structures Limited waste collection in informal settlements; cheap availability of single-use packaging Extended Producer Responsibility legislation not fully enforced; beverage industry practices
Mental Models Belief that individually packaged items are more hygienic; assumption that waste management is solely government responsibility Perception that convenience trumps sustainability; assumption that someone else will clean up waste

By understanding these deeper levels, we can design interventions that address root causes rather than just symptoms.

Core Activity: Iceberg Analysis - Diving Deeper

60-75 minutes
Groups of 4-5
Moderate

Materials Needed:

  • Large paper or cardboard sheets (one per group)
  • Markers or colored pencils
  • Sticky notes or small pieces of paper
  • Optional: Printed photos of local plastic pollution
  • Handout: Iceberg Model template (or students can draw their own)

Activity Steps

1

Introducing the Iceberg (15 minutes)

Begin with a brief demonstration using a real object (like a plastic bottle) or image. Ask students what they can observe about it (the "events" level). Progressively lead them to consider deeper questions:

  • What patterns might we see over time with this item?
  • What systems or structures cause these patterns?
  • What beliefs or values drive these structures?

Explain the four levels of the Iceberg Model using local examples.

2

Group Iceberg Analysis (25 minutes)

Divide students into groups of 4-5. Each group selects a specific plastic waste challenge relevant to their community. Groups create an iceberg diagram on large paper with four levels:

  • Events: What specific incidents of plastic pollution do you see?
  • Patterns: What trends or recurring issues do you notice over time?
  • Structures: What systems, policies, or physical infrastructures create these patterns?
  • Mental Models: What beliefs, values, or assumptions maintain these structures?

Groups use sticky notes to add ideas to each level of their iceberg.

3

Causal Connections (15 minutes)

Introduce the concept of causal loopsâ€”how elements in a system influence each other. Groups draw arrows between different elements on their iceberg to show relationships. Distinguish between:

  • Reinforcing relationships: Where a change in one element amplifies change in another
  • Balancing relationships: Where a change in one element moderates change in another

Identify at least one reinforcing loop and one balancing loop in their system.

4

Solutions at Different Levels (15 minutes)

Groups brainstorm potential interventions at each level of the iceberg. Discuss: Which level might have the most powerful leverage for change? Consider feasibility: Which interventions could your school or community implement? Present key insights and proposed interventions to the class.
