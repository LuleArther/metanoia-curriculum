The Plastic Paradox

The Material That Changed Everything

Plastic has transformed how we live, making products more affordable, accessible, and convenient. Yet this remarkable material has created one of our most urgent environmental challenges.

In Uganda, approximately 600 tons of plastic waste is generated daily in Kampala alone, with only about 6% being properly recycled. Meanwhile, South Africa produces about 2.4 million tons of plastic waste annually, with recycling rates hovering around 14%. Despite the difference in scale, both countries face similar fundamental challenges: growing consumption, inadequate waste management infrastructure, and increasing environmental impacts.

Country Comparison: A Tale of Two Systems

Aspect Uganda South Africa
Population exposure to plastic waste 90% of Kampala residents live within 2km of informal dumpsites 34% of households receive no formal waste collection
Primary plastic challenges Single-use sachets, PET bottles, kaveera bags PET bottles, shopping bags, food packaging
Policy landscape National ban on kaveera bags (though enforcement varies) Plastic bag levy, Extended Producer Responsibility regulations
Informal sector role Waste pickers provide ~80% of recycled material Approximately 60,000 waste pickers recover 80-90% of recycled packaging

Core Activity: Plastic Detective - Following the Trail

Materials needed:

  • Notebook and pen
  • Small plastic collection bag (reused from home)
  • Chart paper or recycled cardboard (one per group)
  • Markers or colored pencils

Materials Alternatives:

Notebook and pen ‚Üí Recycled paper stapled together ‚Üí Banana leaves/large leaves + charcoal for writing ‚Üí Shared recording sheets

Collection bag ‚Üí Reused bread bags or rice sacks ‚Üí Woven basket made from local materials ‚Üí Cloth pouch from old clothing

Chart paper ‚Üí Flattened cardboard boxes ‚Üí Back side of used posters or calendars ‚Üí Banana leaves stitched together

Markers ‚Üí Natural dyes (berries, plants) ‚Üí Charcoal sticks of different thicknesses ‚Üí Clay or mud of different colors

Steps:

  1. Plastic Collection (15 minutes)

    In pairs, collect 5-10 different plastic items found within or near your home or school grounds. For each item, record:

    • What is it?
    • What type of plastic? (look for recycling numbers if available)
    • Where was it found?
    • What was its purpose?
    • What visible signs of age/weathering does it have?

  2. Trace the Journey (15 minutes)

    For each plastic item, work backward to map its possible journey:

    • Where was it likely manufactured?
    • How did it reach your community?
    • Who might have used it?
    • Why was it discarded?
    • Where might it go next?

  3. System Mapping (20 minutes)

    In groups of 4-6, create a visual "plastic journey map" on chart paper:

    • Connect all items collected by the group into one system map
    • Use arrows to show movement of materials
    • Highlight points where plastic "leaks" into the environment
    • Mark potential intervention points where the journey could be changed

  4. Share and Compare (10 minutes)

    Each group presents their map. Identify common patterns across different plastic journeys and discuss differences between types of plastic and their pathways.

Guided Reflection

Take some time to reflect on these questions in your notebook:

Personal Connection

What surprised you most about the plastic you found? How does it connect to your daily life?

Systems Thinking

What patterns did you notice in how plastic moves through your community? Who are the different people involved in this journey?

Comparative Lens

Based on what you've learned about plastic challenges in Uganda and South Africa, what similarities and differences do you notice compared to your own community?

Beginner's Mind

If you were seeing plastic for the very first time, with no preconceptions, how would you describe its value and problems? What questions would you ask?

Emotional Response

How do you feel about what you discovered? Did this activity change how you think about plastic?
