The Power of Not Knowing

Why Unlearning Matters

We often think that knowledge is always an advantage. But sometimes, what we "know" becomes a barrier to innovation. Our assumptions, habits, and established ways of thinking can prevent us from seeing new possibilities. In environmental work, breakthrough solutions often come when we question what everyone takes for granted.

The concept of "beginner's mind" comes from Zen Buddhism, where it's called shoshin. It refers to approaching a subject as if for the first timeâ€”with openness, eagerness, and no preconceptions. As the Zen teacher Shunryu Suzuki put it: "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities; in the expert's mind, there are few."

Beginner's Mind Approach

Instead of saying "I know this already," the beginner's mind approach asks:

  • "What if I've never seen this before?"
  • "What assumptions am I making without realizing it?"
  • "What would a child or someone from a different culture notice?"
  • "What possibilities am I missing because of what I 'know'?"

The Unlearning Process

Unlearning follows a cycle:

1

Awareness

Recognizing our assumptions and established patterns

2

Questioning

Challenging whether these assumptions must be true

3

Experimentation

Trying approaches that contradict our assumptions

4

Reflection

Noticing what happens when we suspend our "knowledge"

5

Integration

Incorporating new possibilities into our thinking

This module will guide you through this unlearning process, specifically focused on our relationship with plastic and waste.

Core Activity: The Assumption Detectives

60-75 minutes
Groups of 4-5
Moderate

Materials Needed:

  • Paper and pens/pencils
  • Sticky notes (if available)
  • A common plastic item (bottle, bag, container, etc.)â€”one per group
  • Optional: Colored markers for mind mapping
Paper and pens alternatives:
  • Leaves and sticks for writing
  • Slate or smooth stone with chalk
  • Oral tradition - verbal mapping with designated "rememberer"
Sticky notes alternatives:
  • Small leaves or petals
  • Stones of different shapes
  • Clay or mud balls to represent different assumptions
Plastic items alternatives:
  • Any common everyday objects (not necessarily plastic)
  • Photos of items if actual items unavailable
  • Verbal descriptions of items (for minimal resource settings)

Activity Steps

1

Assumption Mapping (20 minutes)

Divide into groups of 4-5. Each group receives a common plastic item. Create a mind map of all the assumptions we make about this item. Consider assumptions about:

  • How it's made
  • Why it's designed this way
  • How it should be used
  • What happens after use
  • Who is responsible for it
  • What value it has

Each group should aim to identify at least 15 assumptions.

Example assumption map for a plastic water bottle

2

Assumption Flipping (15 minutes)

Select your 5 strongest assumptions. For each assumption, ask: "What if the opposite were true?"

Example:

Assumption: "Plastic packaging exists to protect products"

Flip: "What if plastic packaging actually harms products?"

Exploration: In what ways might plastic packaging actually reduce product quality or create new problems?

Create a brief written exploration of each flipped assumption.

3

What If? Ideation (15 minutes)

Using your flipped assumptions as inspiration, brainstorm new possibilities for your plastic item. For each flipped assumption, generate at least 2 "What if?" questions.

Example:

Flipped assumption: "What if plastic packaging actually harms products?"

What if? questions:

  • "What if packaging was edible?"
  • "What if products protected packaging instead?"

Select your most promising "What if?" question and develop it into a brief concept.

4

Innovation Showcase (15-20 minutes)

Each group presents their:

  • Original plastic item
  • Key assumptions they identified
  • Most interesting flipped assumptions
  • Favorite "What if?" concept

Discuss how questioning assumptions led to new possibilities. Vote on which concepts seem most novel and which seem most practical.

Try This: Extension Activities

Reverse Assumptions Challenge

Select a common plastic item and list all its typical characteristics. Now reverse each characteristic and explore what new possibilities emerge.

Example:

Typical characteristic: Plastic is lightweight

Reverse: What if packaging were intentionally heavy?

Exploration: Heavy packaging might be reused as building materials, weights, or tools, creating added value beyond containing the product.

Child's Perspective Immersion

Spend 30 minutes observing how young children (ages 3-6) interact with materials. Document their approaches that adults might consider "wrong" or "inefficient." How might these approaches inspire new solutions?

Cross-Cultural Wisdom Mining

Research how three different cultural traditions approached packaging and material storage before plastic. What wisdom might be applicable today?
